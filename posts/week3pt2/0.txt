  <div class="vtbegenerated">
   <p>In 2006, I read a book called "Rule 1 Investing" by Phil Towne.&nbsp; In sum, the book shared a value based (think Warren Buffett) approach to find stocks to invest in. I decided to write my own application that would input a large number of stocks, complete my analysis, and provide a recommendation. (I was sure I'd become wealthy <strong>:)</strong></p> 
   <p>I wrote the application, but when it came time to start analyzing in bulk, I hit a snag.&nbsp; The lowest priced real-time stock prices subscription that I could find would run me a few thousand per year for the raw data I needed. I ended up becoming really good at screen scraping (And in case you're wondering, "Nope, I didn't become wealthy).</p> 
   <p>This evening, I did a similar search on real-time stock price subscription services. Here's my perception based on a quick review of the search results...</p> 
   <p>1. there are more companies offering&nbsp;this type of service</p> 
   <p>2. finding articles comparing stock services is easier, i.e. titles like "Best Free Real-Time Stocks for Day Traders", "API for Free Real-Time Stock Market Data", etc...</p> 
   <p>3. I looked at prices for three companies promoting real-time stock API's.&nbsp; Two of them where charging several hundred per month (same as my experience in 2006).&nbsp; But, one called <a href="https://www.alphavantage.co/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Alpha Advantage</a> looks promising and says they are free.</p> 
   <p>So, in sum my perception is that real-time stock data (and other information about stocks) is more available today than it was ten-plus years ago and is offered by more providers.&nbsp; As for price, maybe it's cheaper too, but you still have to search pretty hard to find a good deal (if there really is one).</p> 
   <p>(Note: this is a biased comparison as I spent days/weeks in 2007 on this research, versus minutes on my search for this discussion).</p>
