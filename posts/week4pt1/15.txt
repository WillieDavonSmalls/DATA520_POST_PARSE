   <p>Understanding drivers for trending topics could help determine ways to deal with the spread of misinformation.&nbsp;&nbsp;The study referenced in the link below concerns COVID-19 and the trending topic that 5G is a cause.&nbsp; The goal of the study was to look for the drivers of the 5G COVID-19 theory.</p> 
   <p>In this study, Twitter data in the U.K. with the #5GCoronavirus hashtag&nbsp;from March 27, 2020 to April 4, 2020 was collected.&nbsp; Influential users were determined using graph clusters and nodes were ranked by betweenness centrality score.&nbsp; Results showed two large networks.&nbsp; One was a group of isolates (users who tweet without any mentions) and the other was a broadcast group (users whose tweets led to retweets).&nbsp; A detailed look at some sample tweets revealed that 65.2% were actually non-supporters of the theory.&nbsp; So, even though it became a trending topic, the majority&nbsp; did not believe the theory that 5G causes COVID-19.&nbsp; In addition, a single Twitter account created only to spread the theory was discovered.&nbsp; No corresponding account to counter the theory was found.&nbsp; The authors believe that users tweeting about the topic jokingly were a major driver in spreading the theory.&nbsp; They propose that an authority or public figure Twitter account actively combating the theory would reduce its spread.&nbsp; They also feel that users should not re-tweet or tweet even in jest, but rather should report it in order to avoid it gaining trending status.&nbsp; (They go on to say that Twitter could then shut down the spread.&nbsp; The topic of whether Twitter and other social media sites should remove content is&nbsp; a whole other discussion.&nbsp;<img src="https://bbhosted.cuny.edu/webapps/vtbe-tinymce/tiny_mce/plugins/emotions/img/smiley-smile.png" alt="Smile" title="Smile" border="0">)&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
