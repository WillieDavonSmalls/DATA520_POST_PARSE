  <div class="vtbegenerated">
   <p>The New York Times posted an OpEd article in early April titled, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/04/05/opinion/coronavirus-google-searches.html" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">"Google Searches Can Help Us Find Emerging COVID-19 Outbreaks"</a>, that highlighted a data scientist's approach to mapping out the spread of COVID-19 throughout the world by mining Google's search data.&nbsp;Through keyword and text analysis for spikes in searches such as "I can't smell",&nbsp;or "my eyes hurt",&nbsp;or specific searches related to fevers, chills, etc. in multiple languages,&nbsp;the author was able to pinpoint emerging&nbsp;hotspots&nbsp;around the same time the confirmed case counts rose in those areas. It's been mentioned before, but Google search term analysis has continued to intrigue data scientists for years, since Google is typically a private way for humans to search the web without having to consult with other people. In this context, it's providing insight into people's anxiety about the virus, and could even help predict where case counts will rise before they actually happen. On top of this, I think it would be interesting to incorporate networks into this type of analysis -- since searches can be subsetted by location and time, networks and graphs could be used to help map the ebbs and flows of those being exposed to the virus. Additionally, based on certain spikes in search terms, it would be interesting to attach edge weights or node attributes that help measure the severity of the hotspots and the trends in Google searches by people in those areas.</p> 
   <p><strong>Business Goals -&nbsp;</strong>the obvious goals of this would be to provide analysis or a visualization tool that could be used to help map and predict where new hotspots&nbsp;are emerging before they actually happen. If there do appear to be trends in searches based on these factors, this could help government officials and local entities combat the virus before it reaches it's full potential in a given hotspot.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p> 
   <p><strong>Potential Sources of Data -&nbsp;</strong>Google Search Terms and any other Google-related keyword searching data. This could also be expanded out to different social networks, i.e. Facebook, Twitter, etc., but would possible fall a bit more outside of the scope of traditional text processing.</p>
